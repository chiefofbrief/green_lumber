# Principles

## Basics

**Be early**: It’s easier to be first than to be smarter. But patience is necessary.  
* Don’t follow the crowd. 
* It’s easier to tell what will happen to the price than how long it will take. 

**Keep it simple**: Complexity makes you miss simple things. 
* Have one strong reason for doing something.
* White out the chart, and focus on the future. 

**Be ignorant**. Ignore all unrelated events and details. 
* Trust intuition. Don’t try to rationalize/explain everything using words.
* Instead of trying to figure out what’s right, remove what’s wrong. 
* Use optionality to hedge against a lack of knowledge. 

**Be antifragile**: Use a barbell approach and optionality to mitigate the risk of black swans. 
* Highly conservative and highly risky. No moderate risks. 
* Spread risk attempts over as many trials as possible.
* Stick to risks where the worst outcome is known and limited.
* Assume every thesis is flawed. Don’t follow a plan, and don’t rely on predictions. 
* Pay attention to what’s static. The longer it’s been around, the less fragile it is. 
* Evaluate the fragility of the underlying system, not the likelihood of an event. 
* Technology displaces itself (and is therefore fragile). 

 **Listen to other people’s ideas**:  The best source of ideas are other growth investors. 
* Technical people (engineers, scientists, etc.) can also be an excellent source. 
* Customers, employees, and competitors are great sources.  
* Always understand peoples’ incentives before listening to their opinions. 
* An idea survives because its messenger did, not because it’s better.

## Target Stocks

**Sales growth is a requirement**: The main difference between the two types of investments is the rate of growth. Profit margin might also be a big difference if younger companies are reinvesting for growth.    

**Healthy balance between growth and risk**: Growth is #1 but risk needs to be taken into account by evaluating competitive advantages, capitalization, and earnings stability. 

**Ideal: Abnormal growth at a reasonable price**:
* Opportunity for major sales growth (and ideally worthwhile profit margins). 
* Competitive advantages such as low costs, IP, distribution (ideally one of the lowest-cost producers).
* Earnings hold up in a down cycle. 
* Price hasn’t caught up yet. Earnings power is underappreciated.
* Compelling story for investors. 
* Size is not a constraint; growth companies can be large or small. 

**Good: Growing and undervalued**: 
* Well established. Ideally the leader in their industry.
* Wide margins. 
* Competitive advantages such as low costs, IP, distribution (ideally one of the lowest-cost producers). 
* Earnings hold up in a down cycle. 
* High switching costs. 
* Underappreciated earnings power. 
* If there are temporary problems, ensure they are temporary and can be solved in months.

## Evaluating Growth

**Market and product potential**: There must be enough market and product potential for major sales growth.
* Ideally in the early to middle stages of an S-curve. 
* Ideally the company has the potential to exceed industry growth as a whole. 
* Might be a producer in a shortage. 
* Happy customers who return. 
* Organic sales growth, not growth by acquisition. 
* Evaluate sales by product segment.
* Evaluate sales growth in units of years (constant YoY increases are not necessary). 

**Profit margin**: The profit margin should provide a cushion against demand troughs, unless money is being reinvested into growth.  
* Ideally among the lowest-cost producers. 
* A low profit margin is acceptable if money is being reinvested into growth (R&D, sales, etc.). 
* Evaluate margin in units of years. 

**Effective R&D**: R&D is among the most cost-intensive activities, especially at rapidly growing companies.  
* Delays and failures are OK, but management must be open about it and adapt. 
* Evaluate profit per dollar of R&D. 

**Capable and determined management with high integrity**: Sales growth depends on management’s skill and willingness to exploit the growth opportunity. 
* Low employee turnover. 
* People effectiveness index = Net profit / Payroll. 
* Management should be open when there are delays, misses, etc.

## Evaluating Risk

**Capitalization**: Commitments (e.g., debt) are manageable, and ideally growth can be self-funded using FCF. 
* Debt / sales 
* High Sales / Assets = High ROI. 

**Margin expansion**: Marginal companies have narrow margins and increase their margins more in good years than low-cost producers.
* Margin troughs.

**Customer concentration**: High customer concentration is risky. 

**Shareholder dilution**: All expenses that dilute shareholders (e.g., SBC) should be treated as cash expenses. 

**One-man show**: Can be a risk if this person’s judgement or availability wanes. 
* Compare the salary of the CEO to other executives.

## Evaluating Price and Timing

**Price is based on the current appraisal of the company’s situation**: The financial community creates an appraisal of the company, which may or may not take into account all facts. More broadly, there are three components of the community’s appraisal: 1) Stocks overall, 2) Industry, 3) Individual stocks. 
* Appraisals can change with or without a change in the business. 
* Every significant price move occurs because the community’s appraisal has changed. 
* Interest rates are the largest factor in the overall appraisal. 
* Analysts are part of the financial community. 

**P/E reflects the amount of anticipated earnings growth**: A stock with growing earnings should sell at a higher P/E than a stable but lower growth stock. 
* The further into the future profits will grow, the higher the P/E you can pay. Rapid earnings growth must be sustained to avoid P/E dropping.
* The P/E that the financial community is willing to pay decreases with maturity. 

**Stocks don’t rise by the same amount**: A run up in the price doesn’t mean it won’t increase more. A depressed price doesn't mean it's due for an increase. 
* Don’t anchor to the historical price range. White out the chart and focus on the future. 

**Don’t worry too much about overpaying (if you buy outstanding companies)**: For outstanding companies, the next peak will surpass the current one. 

**Buy right before a catalyst**: Identify events which will change the appraisal of the stock. 

**Account for Reflexivity**: Sentiment and stock prices can influence a company’s fundamentals, creating self-reinforcing feedback loops where perception shapes reality.

**Account for the Uncertainty premium/discount**: An event’s uncertainty creates a higher/lower price. As time increases, and things become more certain, it erodes.

**Prices usually swing too far in either direction**: This presents risk when the appraisal is favorable, and opportunity when it's unfavorable.

## Portfolio Allocation

**Most funds should go into large growth stocks**: The majority of funds should be allocated to large, established growth stocks as opposed to smaller ones. 

**Industry diversification is necessary**: Especially if it's cyclical. 

**Strive for diversity in product lines**: Some overlap in product lines is OK, but not too much. 

**Diversification within a company increases portfolio diversity**: The more diversity a company has in its product lines, the more diversified the portfolio’s product lines are.
